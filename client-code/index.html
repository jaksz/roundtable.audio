About Roundtable.audio

Since ancient times, humans have relied on spoken language to collaborate, organize, and connect with one another. But in recent times, much of our meaningful dialogue has been locked away in written text on the internet - taking the form of email, social media posts, comment sections, and instant messages. And even when we do speak online, it's often controlled or moderated by one individual or organization. In many ways, we've lost our ability to simply speak with one another at all, causing unprecented political divide and a decline in social relationships.

Roundtable.audio is meant to be the simplest decentralized conversation tool on the internet. We don't expect to fix the entirety of the above problem, but we want to provide a tool that can at least get the conversation going.

To get started, simply create or join a roundtable and let your voice be heard. Or to understand more of what you can do with Roundtable.audio, head over to the FAQ section.

Roundtable.audio is a blend of Node, Golang, and native Javascript. It was built on top of Pion and Kraken - special thanks to these open-source projects/communities.

FAQs

What is Roundtable.fm?

A platform for live, decentralized, broadcasted audio conferencing.

What can I do with it?

Quite a bit. Like a podcast, you can use it as a stage for dialogue. Like a radio station, you can use it to project a message to a wide audience. Roundtables can also be private, meaning they can be used for closed hangouts, meetings, and much more.

What is the distinction between a public and private roundtable?

A roundtable can be:

  • public to join and listen to
  • private to join, but public to listen to
  • private to join and listen to

With these options it provides complete privacy if desired or a powerful stage to project ideas.

What do you mean by "decentralized?"

A roundtable has no moderator. If there are multiple participants, no one person has the ability to mute or kick any one person for the rest of the group. This removes any power dynamic and creates a balance of power between all participants. It has nothing to do with blockchain technology if that's what you thought we meant (at least not yet).

Is it free? Do I have to register?

It is completely free. At the moment, no registration is required to participate in or listen to a roundtable.

Are roundtables stored? Can I listen to one after it ends?

No. Roundtables are live-only, meaning that once a conversation is over, it's over. We believe this takes the pressure off of folks who are eager to share ideas and opinions but afraid of "going-on record." Of course, we cannot stop people from attempting to record the audio through alternative means, so be wary of this.

Are Roundtables monitored? Am I being listened to by the creators of roundtable.fm in a private roundtable?

No and no. We are placing trust in you to use roundtable.fm with respect, and to follow the community standards/guidlines found in the Philosophy section.

Was this created in response to Covid-19? Or the current political climate?

Sort of. The original idea was actually conceived in the Fall of 2019 as a way to combat boredom on a long commute, but when lockdown began it was clear this would be a great time to build it. It was made even more apparent in the heat of the political turmoil later in the year. Now, more than ever, people need to be speaking to one another.

Roundtable.audio Philosophy
Roundtable.audio is meant to be a welcoming environment. People should be able to speak freely and share their opinions, learn new things, and enjoy the company of others. Roundtable are not recorded or stored, which removes some of the pressure often associated with written text in online forums. Additionally, the decentralized nature of a roundtable - having no moderator - should allow for more free-form discussion without fear of being shut-down. We think these two elements will empower healthy roundtable and fun conversations. However, with this power, autonomy, and anonymity, we require community buy-in and respect to make it work.

We therefore ask you to do your part and follow some guidelines:
  • You may never create roundtables which are meant to threaten, disparage, or target individuals or groups. We ask that if you see such a case, please report it to info@roundtable.fm.
  • When in a roundtable, hate speech is not tolerated in any way, shape or form. We ask that if you are in a roundtable where such speech is occurring to leave the roundtable and encourage others to do so. If you'd like to keep the conversation going, create a new private roundtable without that participant. We think this is a healthier way to discourage this type of behavior without giving centralized power to a moderator.
  • Give others the chance to be heard if there is more than one participant. Everyone who has a seat at the table should feel encouraged and included to be a part of the discussion.
If you do your part, we'll do ours. We want to continue to improve the experience of the community and introduce features that empower and encourage meaningful, respectful dialogue. If you have any ideas for improving community standards, we're all ears at info@roundtable.audio.
